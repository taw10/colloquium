Welcome to Colloquium!
======================

###### Slide 0; resource:///uk/me/bitwiz/colloquium/banner.svg

It looks like this is the first time you've used Colloquium.  Keep reading to understand a little bit about how Colloquium works and how to use it.


Motivation
----------

You've probably spent a long time using traditional presentation software, or *slideware*, which almost entirely focuses on visual aids (slides).  The results might be visually stunning, but a clear, confident and within-time presentation takes more than just graphics.  Colloquium aims to give you these missing tools.

There's a big problem here.  Human beings cannot follow two streams of language at once.  If the audience are listening to your spoken words, they cannot simultaneously read the text on the screen.  And if they read what's on the screen, they will not hear what you are saying.

So, why do so many presentations ask the audience to do both at the same time?  It's because traditional slideware strongly encourages you to work this way.  In most slideware, the first thing you see on creating a new presentation is a slide template with invitations to add bullet points.  In fact, the concept has permeated right down to the word "presentation".  We *present* something (slides) instead of *speaking*, *lecturing* or *educating* – with or without visual aids.

Colloquium breaks this dependency on slides by giving you a workspace (this window) to work on the flow of your speech.  Whatever you write here will later become a kind of autocue when you give your presentation.  Visual aids can be inserted at appropriate points, and will be displayed on the external screen (projector) at the right time.

You can use this workspace in whatever way is right for you, whether it's short notes, bullet points or a word-for-word script.  The important part is that you focus on the narrative of your talk, figure out what you're trying to say.

The best part is that your audience won't notice any difference.  You can continue to use whatever design template is required by your company.  You can even continue to use the same software to design your slides, before importing them into Colloquium. The only difference will be a big improvement in the flow, timing and clarity of your talk!


Keeping time
------------

While you're presenting, Colloquium's _presentation clock_ (find it in the Tools menu) estimates how far through the text you should be, based on the elapsed time and the number of words.  It warns you if you're running ahead of and behind this estimate (with colours, green or red respectively), and by how much (by the length of the bar chart).

Writing a presentation
----------------------

To start creating your presentation, select File→New and simply type into the window as you would into a text editor or word processor.

To add a new slide or other kind of visual aid, just drag and drop the image files into this window, use the "Add" button in the toolbar, or Insert→Slide in the menu bar.  Supported formats include PNG, SVG and PDF, with more to be added in the future.

Double-clicking an image will open a new _slide view window_, which will become important when giving the presentation (see later).

Note that the graphics can have different sizes and aspect ratios.  Colloquium will take care of displaying them at an appropriate size, letterboxing when necessary.

Colloquium shows an estimated running time for your presentation at the bottom of this window.  This estimate depends on how fast you speak, but also on how detailed your narrative is — whether you make a word-for-word script, or shorter notes.  You can change the calibration for this in the preferences window (tab "Presentation").


Giving the presentation
-----------------------

First, open as many slide views as you need.  You might only need one, usually full-screen on a projector, but it's often useful to have a second window on your personal screen.  Colloquium has no "presenter view" mode to take over your screen.  Instead, you should lay out Colloquium's windows, and any others you might need, on your private display to match your own requirements.

Double-click any image or slide to open a window, or use Tools→"Open new slide window" in the menu bar.  Double-click a slide window to make it full-screen.

You can directly open a full-screen slide view on a certain screen. Use Tools→"Open new full-screen slide window on display", and select the correct video output.  Colloquium will not alter your computer's graphics configuration in any way, so if something doesn't show up correctly in this list, fix the problem via your system's display settings.

Now, select Tools->"Start presenting from here" in the menu bar.  This will switch Colloquium into _presentation mode_, where your position in the narrative controls which slide is displayed by the slide view windows.

Use page up\/page down or the left\/right arrow keys to move through the text as you speak it out.  The current paragraph will be highlighted.  When you advance past a slide's thumbnail, that slide will be displayed in _all_ slide view windows.  To jump to a slide, double-click on its thumbnail in this window.

To leave presentation mode, click the button in the toolbar.  To restore a full-screen window to normal, press Escape with the window focused.  Note that leaving presentation mode does not automatically close any of the slide views.


Wrapping up
-----------

That's more than enough to get started.  Once you get going, take a look at some of the other features including the _test card_ and _segments_, and the options for customizing the appearance of the narrative workspace.

We hope you enjoy using Colloquium.  Now go and give some great presentations!
